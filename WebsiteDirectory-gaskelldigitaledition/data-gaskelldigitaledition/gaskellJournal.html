Dedication

Gaskell dedicates her diary to her firstborn daughter, Marianne, who was born September 12, 1834. At the time of Gaskell’s death, Marianne was yet unmarried. She later married and had several children; the diary has been handed down through Marianne’s descendants, and is currently the property of Mrs. Sarah Prince, Marianne’s great-great granddaughter.

Entry 1

March 10, 1835

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 6 months

Elapsed Time: Not applicable

Length of Entry: 10 handwritten pages

In this first entry, Gaskell devotes a lengthy portion of her narrative (3 full pages) to writing about her own maternal choices - in this case, particularly her plans for handling common parenting concerns, including bedtimes, how to respond to cries and demands, and developmental milestones like learning to walk. This keen awareness of motherhood is marked by self-doubt and an equally pronounced sense of self-judgment stemming from Victorian assumptions about maternal duty. Gaskell uses the diary as a means to check her own maternal behaviors, writing: though I keep laying down rules, I fear I have not sufficiently attended to them, though I hope I have been conscientious hitherto in discharging my duty to her. These rules draw in part on educational treatises of the day; in this entry she cites a text by the French authoress Madame Necker DeSaussure. Gaskell’s conclusions, however, are more rigid for herself than for Marianne. While insisting that Marianne not be pressured into walking or learning anything before she is ready, Gaskell repeatedly reinforces her commitment to her own duty to raise Marianne according to strict principles, such as telling her nothing but the absolute truth and selflessly placing her own needs after those of her daughter.

The dedication itself, where Gaskell hopes the diary will be reserved for [Marianne] as a token of her mother’s love and extreme anxiety in the formation of her little daughter’s character, demonstrates even at the outset the depth of introspection that Gaskell associates with the diary form, and with this manuscript in particular. Although undertaken to record Marianne’s character in it’s earliest form, the diary is ostensibly also a record of Gaskell’s own maternal anxieties, and in being so, it is also suggests her fears of maternal mortality, which echo as an underlying theme throughout the diary, both in her repeated fears of loss and in her inclusion of prayers for her daughters’ safety. The brief God bless her that closes this entry marks a tendency to conclude her entries with prayers for her daughters’ health and safety that continues throughout the diary. At all times, the relationship between mother and daughters is colored by a pervasive fear of death.

Gaskell’s first entry is slightly over ten handwritten pages, and was likely written on the same date as the dedication; the handwriting is similar in size, saturation and ink color. Gaskell appears to have often written in the evenings when her children were in bed; this and several other entries indicate evening in the dateline. Gaskell writes in this entry about Marianne’s bedtime routine; she was laid down awake, at 6pm every evening, and since the entry concludes with a comment about listening to Marianne’s restful breathing during the time of writing, it seems likely that this entry was written sometime after 6 pm.

August 4, 1835

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 11 months

Length of Entry: Seven handwritten pages

The second entry follows the pattern Gaskell established at the outset of simultaneously celebrating Marianne’s accomplishments and fearing her loss. Gaskell has carefully delineated her objectives for raising Marianne, many of which emphasize allowing her to develop and learn at her own pace. To this end, Gaskell recounts that Marianne recognizes many words and individuals, but does not walk or talk. Gaskell also continues with the theme of maternal responsibility, her asserting rules that she tr[ies] to attend to & make the servants attend to. The cheerful mood that begins the entry fades as Gaskell reflects on an attack of croup from which Marianne has recently recovered. She recounts her fear of losing her daughter, which was no doubt strengthened by the memory of the stillbirth of her firstborn daughter fourteen months before Marianne’s birth.
Note: (Resp:#mjk) The stillbirth occurred in July of 1833; at the time of this entry it had been just over two years since this loss. The fear is quite poignant for Gaskell, and she copies a portion of a poem by Caroline Bowles, To A Dying Infant, which was frequently used as a Victorian epitaph. The entry includes a prayer of thankfulness that Marianne was spared, which falls in this case mid-entry.

Entry 3

October 4, 1835

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 13 months

Elapsed Time: Two months since previous entry

Length of Entry:

At the time she wrote this entry, Marianne was the same age that Gaskell herself had been when she lost her own mother. The entry is preoccupied with Gaskell’s observations and decisions related to Marianne’s physical health. She introduces her belief that in children, every change of temper might be deduced from some corresponding change in the body. She cites Combe’s Physiology as one source of this conviction and describes in detail Marianne’s temperament. In addition to offering examples of Marianne’s self-government and her improvement in patience, Gaskell also lays out again her mother-rules for raising Marianne. Due to Marianne’s fragility, Gaskell plans to dress her warmly and keep her indoors during the winter, and she has stopped feeding Marianne milk, believing that thickened broth is a more strengthening food. The entry concludes with a lengthy prayer asking not only for Marianne’s health, but for Gaskell’s own strength in the event that she should lose her only daughter.

Entry 4

December 28, 1835

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 15 months

Elapsed Time: Two and a half months since the previous entry.

Length of Entry: Six handwritten pages

Gaskell continues describing Marianne’s development and her own convictions about parenting in this entry. According to the descriptions given here, the Gaskells were still calling Marianne baby. She would be an only child until 1837. Gaskell both celebrates behaviors that she interprets as the beginning of self-government, while also commenting at length on Marianne’s recovery from an illness that is recounted in the previous entry. She mentions throughout Marianne’s personality- that she recognizes family, will share food, rarely cries, etc., and mentions her growing vocabulary and general observation and attentiveness. Throughout the entry, in addition to learning more about Marianne - she still does not walk, and is still living on thickened broth, which Gaskell believes has improved her health - we continue to learn more about Gaskell’s maternal instincts. She is deeply self-critical and sets high standards for herself. In the opening of the entry, she criticizes herself for not having written sooner, although it has been only two months since the previous entry. She also claims that she ought not to be negligent in any regard toward her daughter, including in this instance, in writing about her in the journal. Of particular note in this entry is the penultimate sentence, in which Gaskell writes, I feel weak & exhausted with writing, or I had meant to write more. This admission, in addition to underscoring the duty Gaskell feels to keep the diary, foregrounds the weight which maternal writing held for Gaskell. This writing is not merely record keeping or reflection; it is a deeply personal, emotionally invested act which requires energy of the author. Even paper motherhood is taxing. The entry, as many do, ends with a prayer, asking a blessing upon Marianne, and help for Gaskell as she fulfils her sense of duty.

February 7, 1836

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 17 months

Elapsed Time: Five weeks since the previous entry

Length of Entry: Nearly six handwritten pages

Although this is not the last entry in which Gaskell writes only about Marianne, it is the last one in which she has only one child. By the date of the following entry, Gaskell was already expecting her second daughter, Margaret Emily (called Meta) . Six of the eleven journal entries, including this one, are written on Sundays, usually in the evenings. Gaskell opens this entry with a recollection of the sermon text, drawn from the Christmas narrative in Luke, which mentions a mother treasuring memory of her son
Note: (Resp:) See textual note regarding the placement of this reading in February. Gaskell connects the text with her own maternal practice of actively seeking to remember her children, and specifically credits her diary-keeping practice: I sometimes think I may find this little journal a great help in recalling the memory of my darling child if we should lose her.

After this rather melancholy opening, Gaskell goes on to record Marianne’s development according to her custom: first physical development, and then social and emotional. Between the lines of the text, however, Gaskell has inscribed another record, the record of her own re-reading of the journal. Beyond merely suggesting that rereading the journal would be helpful in the instance of Marianne’s death, Gaskell actually rereads her diary while she is living, as indicated by the sentence, I see I have generally begun my journal with describing the bodily progress she has made, and I will keep to the proper order of things. Thus, even in writing the diary itself, Gaskell is prescribing and following maternal rules. The diary too, must follow a carefully planned form in order to be successful. It must be proper.

Gaskell is on the whole pleased with Marianne’s development here, claiming that her personality promises to be affectionate and pleasant, although she bemoans her disobedience. The remaining diary entry emphasizes obedience on two levels: Marianne’s disobedience in trying to traverse a gap between two chairs, resulting in a fall, and Gaskell’s own disobedience to her perceived higher calling as a mother. Gaskell ends this entry with a rather lengthy prayer element, in which she bemoans the sin that doth so easily beset me, anger, as the rest of the entry would suggest, and prays for help to control herself, particularly for her daughter. She “intends” herself to be patient, just as she intends Marianne to be independent, and as in much of the journal, she takes the responsibility herself, though calling on a higher power to support her goals.

Entry 6

November 5, 1836

Children's Ages: Marianne, aged 26 months old; Gaskell was expecting Meta at the time.

Elapsed Time: Nine months have passed since the last entry.

Length of Entry: Eleven and a half handwritten pages.

The sixth entry foregrounds Gaskell’s belief that Marianne’s behavior is heavily dependent upon and affected by her physiological status. She follows her established pattern, beginning with a description of Marianne’s physical development, followed by her intellectual and moral development. The entry’s physical description surrounds Marianne’s learning to walk, which she did at the late age of 22 months old. According to Gaskell, upon the advice of Dr. Richard Deane, a partner in her Uncle Peter Holland’s medical practice in Knutsford, Marianne was kept from being put on her feet during a summer visit in 1836, when she would have been nearly 21 months old. Following a “violent attack” which the Gaskells treated with a warm bath and a dose of castor oil, Marianne cut her eye teeth, and subsequently began almost suddenly to walk. The narrative of this milestone step exemplifies Gaskell’s attitudes toward development throughout the diary: while eager to see Marianne’s achievements, Gaskell insists on letting her daughters develop at their own pace, and concerns herself primarily with helping Marianne to develop habits of perseverance and patience, rather than insisting on her gaining any particular knowledge or skills.

The remainder of the diary entry emphasizes Marianne’s intellectual and moral development; she enjoys stories and chatters often, but Gaskell, as with walking, intends to wait on formal instruction until Marianne is over 3 years old. She is pleased with her daughter’s awareness of right and wrong, commenting that Marianne often talks to herself about being a dood dirl [good girl], and even recording a situation in which she observed an adult engaged in something wrong and told her mother about it. Although she shows evident pleasure in Marianne’s growth, Gaskell again closes the entry with anxious maternal worries. Recalling the unexpected death of an acquaintance’s only child, she concludes with the observation of how very precious may the remembrance of [events recorded in the journal] become, and a prayer for her strength and resignation in the face of possible loss.

The seventh entry spans twelve handwritten manuscript pages, one of the longest entries in the diary. In this entry Gaskell introduces two new individuals; her second daughter, Margaret Emily, called Meta, and her maternal aunt, Gaskell’s adoptive more-than-mother Hannah Lumb. Aunt Lumb was a *widowed woman who raised Gaskell after her mother’s death when she was only thirteen months old. Shortly following the birth of Meta, Aunt Lumb suffered a stroke that marked the beginning of her final illness. Marianne had been staying with her at the time, during her mother’s confinement, and both Gaskell and one-month-old Meta traveled to be with Marianne and Aunt Lumb early in March until her death in May. These events, Meta’s birth and Aunt Lumb’s death, are alluded to by Gaskell early in the entry, when she writes that “sad excuses” in some part account for the year-long lapse between this diary entry and the one that preceded it.

Much of the entry is told looking back on the months that have passed between the entries, during which time Meta was born, and Aunt Lumb passed away. The events were closely connected, in fact; Marianne had gone to stay with Aunt Lumb had a paralytic stroke, and Gaskell traveled to Knutsford, with newborn Meta and a servant, Betsy, and remained there in rented rooms with both girls for nearly two months until Aunt Lumb’s death on May 1st. A letter, dated 18 March 1837, recounts Gaskell’s experience of Aunt Lumb’s illness. There is also a second letter, written to Holbrook Gaskell, tentatively dated in 1837 as Marianne is the only child mentioned; part of the letter is missing. The letter details plans for the possibility of negotiating a new servant.

The diary entry looks back on these events and comments tenderly on Aunt Lumb’s care of both Marianne and Gaskell, and recounts Gaskell’s careful management of the situation with Marianne, who was not told that Aunt Lumb had died. The later part of the entry discusses the differences between the two sisters, commenting that Meta, already ten months old, is livelier and physically developing earlier than Marianne, crawling “famously”. Marianne, to Gaskell’s pleasure, has a very “acute” conscience, and is becoming “useful and independent,” often willing to share with her sister and generally obedient and cheerful. Meta, however, is saucy, clever, and not so gentle as her elder sister.

In the dateline of this entry, the year is obscured, so that an 8 is superimposed over the seven. As the entry follows an entry for December 1837, however, and the entirety of the diary continues in chronological sequence, it is clear that this entry was written in 1838. Although Meta is at this time over a year old, Gaskell appears rushed in this entry and writes only about Marianne, who is to begin Infant School. Gaskell’s maternal responsibility surrounding the education of her daughters is a fitting topic in the wake of Queen Victoria’s accession to the throne nine months earlier, in June 1837. The coronation had not yet taken place, but the country must have been watching closely as they prepared for rule under a female monarch. The entry foregrounds Gaskell’s indecisiveness or doubt in maternal choices, and she uses the journal to weigh both sides of the decision. On one hand, she worries, Marianne may learn things at school from the other children that she would not wish her to know, and may care less for her home, but on the other hand, Gaskell reasons, school will give her the opportunity to learn obedience and perseverance, which are characteristics that Gaskell and her husband are firmly devoted to. They are not concerned with Marianne’s learning much in the school, so much as her developing the necessary habits that will underly her learning later.

The entry builds upon Gaskell’s concerns with Marianne’s education by discussing her initial mother-daughter conversations regarding religious belief. Marianne has attended her first Chapel service, on Christmas Day in 1837, at age 3, and Gaskell is pleased with her understanding of the teachings so far. Marianne’s language is developing; she knows her letters and can spell a few words, and recite a poem.

The entry’s closing allows an intriguing glimpse into the connections Gaskell makes between this early diary and her later writing career. In the final paragraph, where she bemoans the lack of time to write anything about Meta, Gaskell writes pointedly, I must devote my next ‘chapter’ to her. This is the first moment in the diary in which she likens her writing to its commercial counterpart. This connection notably follows Gaskell’s first publication. The previous year, in January 1837, only a month before Meta’s birth, Sketches Among The Poor, a poem co-written by the Gaskells, had appeared in Blackwoods’.

The ninth entry draws comparisons between the two daughters, and is particularly concerned with health, and again, with the possibility of loss. Gaskell opens the entry by recounting her fright at Marianne’s recent attack of croup, which likely occurred only days after the previous entry. This illness was deeply significant for Gaskell; it forms the primary substance of a contemporaneous letter, dated March 30, 1838 to Eliza (Aunt Lizzy) Gaskell, William Gaskell’s then unmarried sister, and one of Gaskell’s most frequent early correspondents. Gaskell also comments in both sources (diary and letter) about the death of Eddy Deane, whose father, Dr. Richard Deane, was Gaskell’s uncle Peter Holland’s partner in Knutsford. Eddy Deane was nine months older than Marianne and had been ill with croup the same night as Marianne and died four days later. GGaskell interjects a prayer for resignation into the account following this story.

This entry marks another instance of Gaskell’s own rereading of her journal. As she turns from recording Marianne’s illness to fulfill her promise of devoting the next chapter to Meta, she writes I have been amused on looking at my former writing in this book to perceive the difference between the two little girls. Gaskell has done this before, commenting on her own perception of the diary as she rereads it before continuing its composition.

This is the shortest entry in the diary, barely covering two handwritten pages. In the entry which follows, Gaskell states that she had been interrupted when she last wrote, which in part explains the brevity of this entry. Gaskell’s focus here is on comparing her daughters; Meta once again is described as much the most energetic and constantly busy, whereas Gaskell worries that Marianne is not industrious and requires guidance to keep busy. Marianne has improved however, and Gaskell is pleased to see that her former obstinacy is growing less frequent. Even as she comments on her daughters’ achievements and cautiously observes their faults, however, Gaskell takes on herself the responsibility: I am afraid I do not set her a good example, she writes of Marianne’s lack of initiative. In spite of this however, Marianne has begun to read and sew, though she is only four years old. The entry ends rather abruptly after Gaskell asserts that she still ascribes much of Marianne’s behavior to physical complaints; she makes a consistent effort to keep her quiet, as otherwise she suffers much from exhaustion which shows itself in increased irritability. Here the entry ends.

The final entry in the diary is still quite brief, although at the outset Gaskell states that she intends to make up for having been interrupted in the previous entry. She continues to worry about Marianne’s health, and writes that they are traveling to Prospect Hill, the home of a relative of William’s, south of Warrington, on the recommendation of William’s brother, Sam Gaskell, who was a physician and seems to have been a frequent purveyor of medical advice to the family. Marianne is progressing in other respects, as well, and has begun to have regular lessons at home in reading and sewing. Gaskell prays for patience in regulating her temper during these lessons. While she applauds Marianne’s sympathy for others, she is still concerned about what she believes is a lack of perseverance.
